What a Real COA Includes — and How to Spot a Fake

Published 2026-06-30  ·  By TrustCOA

The difference between a legitimate Certificate of Analysis and a fabricated one often comes down to a handful of specific fields. This guide walks through each section of a real COA, explains what the test methods actually mean, and gives you a practical checklist for identifying altered documents.

The Six Sections of a Legitimate COA

A complete Certificate of Analysis from an accredited third-party laboratory contains six identifiable sections. Each serves a distinct purpose, and the absence of any one of them should prompt scrutiny.

1

Laboratory Header and Identity

The top of a legitimate COA identifies the issuing laboratory by its full legal name, physical address, phone number, and accreditation number. This is not the brand or manufacturer — it is the independent analytical laboratory that performed the testing. The accreditation number links to a verifiable entry in a public registry (such as A2LA or PJLA for ISO/IEC 17025-accredited labs in the United States). If the "laboratory" listed is the same company selling the product, the document is not an independent COA.

2

Sample Identification

This section identifies what was tested: the product or compound name using standard chemical nomenclature, the lot number or batch number assigned by the manufacturer, a laboratory-assigned sample ID, and typically the name and address of the client who submitted the sample. The lot number in this section is the one that must match the lot number printed on the physical product label exactly.

3

Testing Dates

Two dates matter: the date the laboratory received the sample, and the date testing was completed (sometimes called the analysis date or report date). The receipt date must precede the analysis date. Both should be consistent with the manufacturer's stated production timeline. A COA dated before the product could plausibly have been manufactured is a significant anomaly.

4

Analytical Methods

Each test result on a legitimate COA is tied to the specific analytical method used to produce it. Without named methods, a result is an assertion, not a measurement. The section below covers the methods most commonly referenced on research compound COAs.

5

Results Table with Specifications

The results table lists each analyte tested (purity, identity, moisture, residual solvents, heavy metals, microbial counts, endotoxin, etc.), the measured result, the unit of measurement, the specification limit, and an explicit pass or fail determination. The specification is the acceptable threshold set in advance. The result is what the instrument actually measured. A document that shows only pass/fail without the measured values, or shows results without specifications to compare against, is incomplete.

6

Authorized Signature

A legitimate COA is signed by a qualified laboratory scientist — the person responsible for the accuracy of the reported data. The signature should include the signer's name and title. Some laboratories use auditable electronic signatures with timestamp and identity verification; this is acceptable provided the electronic signature system is compliant with applicable standards (e.g., 21 CFR Part 11 for FDA-regulated contexts). An unsigned document is not a complete COA.

Understanding the Analytical Methods Named on a COA

The method citation on a COA is not boilerplate — it is the single piece of information that allows an independent reviewer to evaluate whether the measurement was conducted with appropriate rigor. Here are the methods most commonly referenced in research compound COAs:

Method What it measures Why it matters
HPLC Purity (% of target compound vs. impurities) High-Performance Liquid Chromatography separates compounds and quantifies each peak. The target compound's peak area relative to all peaks gives the purity percentage. Standard method for purity in supplement and research compound COAs.
LC-MS / LC-MS/MS Molecular identity confirmation Liquid Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry confirms that the compound's measured molecular mass matches the target compound. LC-MS/MS adds a second fragmentation stage for more definitive structural identification. Purity without identity confirmation cannot rule out a high-purity sample of the wrong compound.
ICP-MS Heavy metal content (arsenic, cadmium, lead, mercury) Inductively Coupled Plasma-Mass Spectrometry measures trace elemental concentrations at parts-per-billion levels. Required for heavy metal testing in supplements and necessary for materials where raw material sourcing may introduce metallic contaminants.
Karl Fischer Titration Moisture / water content A precise electrochemical method for quantifying water in a sample. Relevant for lyophilized (freeze-dried) research compounds where moisture content affects stability and concentration calculations.
USP <85> LAL / rFC Bacterial endotoxin (lipopolysaccharide) Limulus Amebocyte Lysate (LAL) and recombinant Factor C (rFC) assays detect endotoxins from gram-negative bacteria. Critical for injectable or reconstituted research compounds. Results reported in EU/mL.
GC-MS / GC-FID Residual solvents Gas Chromatography with Mass Spectrometry or Flame Ionization Detection identifies and quantifies trace solvents remaining from synthesis. ICH Q3C residual solvent limits apply to pharmaceutical-grade materials.

How to Read a Results Table

The results table is the core data section of the COA. Each row typically contains: the analyte name; the test method; the specification (the acceptable limit or range); the result (the actual measured value); and a pass/fail determination.

For example, a purity row might read: Purity | HPLC | ≥ 98.0% | 98.7% | PASS. The specification says the material must be at least 98.0% pure. The instrument measured 98.7%. The result exceeds the specification, so it passes.

A result that reads 0.00 ppm for every heavy metal analyte — rather than values that vary at trace levels — may indicate the table was filled in manually rather than populated from instrument data. Real instrumental measurements rarely produce uniformly zero results across an entire metals panel.

Common Fake COA Tactics — and How to Identify Them

COA fraud in the supplement and research compound space has become more sophisticated as buyers have become more demanding. Here are the patterns most frequently observed:

  • Copied letterhead What it looks like: The COA uses the name and logo of a real, accredited laboratory, but the document was not actually issued by that lab. The contact information may be slightly different, or the accreditation number may not be current. How to catch it: Call the laboratory directly using a number sourced from the accrediting body's registry, not the COA, and ask whether they issued a specific report number for a specific lot.
  • Mismatched lot numbers What it looks like: The lot number on the COA differs from the lot number on the physical product label — different digits, different prefix, or the label has no lot number at all. How to catch it: Compare the two directly. A single character difference is a mismatch, not a close match. A passing COA from a different lot number says nothing about the lot you have.
  • Missing methods What it looks like: The results table lists purity at 99.2% but does not state whether this was measured by HPLC, UV spectrophotometry, or some other technique. Why it matters: Without a named method, the number is unverifiable. Different methods have different selectivities and detection limits, and a purity result from a less rigorous method may not reflect actual compound content.
  • No accreditation What it looks like: The COA is issued by a laboratory that lists no ISO 17025 accreditation number, or lists a number that cannot be found in any accrediting body's public registry. Why it matters: An unaccredited laboratory has not been independently assessed for measurement competence, calibration traceability, or measurement uncertainty. Results from unaccredited labs cannot be independently validated.
  • Edited PDF What it looks like: A real COA from a legitimate lab, with values altered — typically purity increased or failed analytes changed to passing. How to catch it: Check PDF metadata for a modification date after the document issue date. Look for font inconsistencies in the results rows. Contact the laboratory to confirm the specific result values on file for that report number.
  • Reused document What it looks like: A legitimate COA from a previous lot is presented as documentation for a current, different lot — either because the current lot was never tested, or because the current lot failed testing. How to catch it: The lot number mismatch is the tell. If the lot on the COA matches a previous batch, it is not documentation for the current one, regardless of whether both batches are the same compound.

COA Verification Checklist

Use this checklist when evaluating any Certificate of Analysis for a research compound or supplement:

  • ✓Laboratory header names a specific accredited third-party lab — not the manufacturer or brand.
  • ✓Accreditation number is present and can be verified in the relevant body's public registry (A2LA, PJLA, or equivalent).
  • ✓Lot number on the COA exactly matches the lot number on the physical product label.
  • ✓Sample receipt date precedes the analysis/report date. Both are consistent with the production timeline.
  • ✓An analytical method is named for each key result (HPLC for purity, LC-MS/MS for identity, ICP-MS for heavy metals, etc.).
  • ✓Results table shows both the specification limit and the actual measured result for each analyte — not only pass/fail.
  • ✓Numeric results across the metals panel do not all show exactly 0.00 — trace variability is normal in real instrument output.
  • ✓Document is signed by a named, titled laboratory scientist.
  • ✓PDF metadata modification date matches or precedes the document issue date (no post-issue editing).
  • ✓Laboratory contact information can be independently verified via the accrediting body's registry or the lab's verified website — not only via details printed on the COA.
  • ✓COA data is accessible at a permanent, brand-hosted URL (not solely as a PDF file distributed by the seller).

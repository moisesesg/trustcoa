How to Verify a Certificate of Analysis (COA): A Complete Guide

Published 2026-06-30  ·  By TrustCOA

A Certificate of Analysis is only as useful as your ability to confirm it is authentic, unaltered, and tied to the exact batch you have in hand. This guide walks through every step of that process.

What Is a Certificate of Analysis?

A Certificate of Analysis (COA) is a formal document issued by an analytical laboratory reporting the test results for a specific batch of material. In the supplement, nutraceutical, and research compound industries, a COA is the primary way a brand communicates to a buyer what is actually in a product — and in what quantity — as measured by an independent instrument, not claimed by the manufacturer.

A COA is batch-specific. Each production run or lot receives its own COA drawn from representative samples of that run. A COA from a previous lot does not speak to the current one.

Who Issues a Legitimate COA?

Legitimate COAs are issued by accredited, independent third-party analytical laboratories — not by the brand or manufacturer selling the product. Self-issued documents from manufacturers are not COAs in any meaningful sense; they are manufacturer's specifications, which serve a different purpose.

The internationally recognized competence standard for analytical testing laboratories is ISO/IEC 17025. A laboratory accredited to ISO 17025 has been independently assessed for technical competence, calibration traceability, and measurement uncertainty by an accrediting body. In the United States, relevant accrediting bodies include A2LA (American Association for Laboratory Accreditation) and PJLA (Perry Johnson Laboratory Accreditation). Both maintain public, searchable registries of accredited labs.

You can verify a laboratory's accreditation status by looking up its accreditation number on the relevant body's registry directly — not by trusting the number printed on the COA itself.

The Fields Every Real COA Must Include

Understanding what a complete COA looks like is the first line of defense against altered or incomplete documents. Here are the required fields and what to look for in each:

Field What it should contain
Product / compound name The full chemical name or standard nomenclature of the compound tested. Abbreviated names alone are insufficient.
Lot number / batch number The unique identifier assigned to this specific production run. Must match the number on the physical label exactly.
Sample receipt date When the laboratory received the sample for testing. Should precede the test date.
Test date / report date When the testing was completed. Must be on or after receipt date; must not post-date production by an implausible margin.
Laboratory identity Full legal name, physical address, and contact information of the issuing lab. Verifiable independently.
Accreditation number The ISO 17025 (or equivalent) accreditation number. Verifiable via the accrediting body's public registry.
Analytical method The specific instrument and technique used for each analyte — e.g., HPLC (High-Performance Liquid Chromatography) for purity, LC-MS or LC-MS/MS for identity confirmation, ICP-MS for heavy metals.
Results table Each analyte tested, its measured value, unit of measurement (mg/mL, %, ppm, etc.), and specification limit.
Pass / fail determination An explicit statement for each analyte whether the result meets or fails the specification.
Authorized signature The name, title, and signature (or auditable digital equivalent) of the laboratory scientist responsible for the report.

How to Confirm a COA Matches the Batch You Have

Step one is physical: compare the lot number printed on the COA against the lot number on the physical label of your product. These must match character-for-character. A lot number that is close but not exact — a transposed digit, a different prefix — is not a match.

Step two is independent verification. Contact the issuing laboratory directly using contact information you source independently (from the accrediting body's registry or the lab's verified website), not from the COA document itself. Ask them to confirm the report number and lot number are on file. Reputable accredited labs will confirm or deny issuance of a specific report number.

Step three, where available, is to use the laboratory's own report verification portal, or a hosted verification platform. Some accredited labs offer a URL or portal where a report number can be entered to confirm authenticity. Red Flags of Altered or Fake COAs

COA fraud in supplement and research compound supply chains typically falls into three categories: recycled documents from a previous lot, copied laboratory letterhead with fabricated results, and edited PDFs where legitimate results have been modified. Here are the warning signs:

  • ⚠ Lot number mismatch. The number on the COA does not exactly match the number on the physical label. This is the most common and most consequential discrepancy.
  • ⚠ Test date inconsistency. The testing date predates the manufacturer's stated production or fill date, or is implausibly recent for a batch already in wide distribution.
  • ⚠ Unverifiable accreditation. The accreditation number listed cannot be found in the relevant accrediting body's public registry, or the lab's address listed on the COA does not match official registry records.
  • ⚠ No analytical method named. A legitimate COA names the method for each key analyte (HPLC, LC-MS/MS, Karl Fischer titration, etc.). A document that lists results without stating how they were measured provides no verifiable basis for those results.
  • ⚠ Suspiciously round numbers. Real instrumental measurements rarely produce perfectly round values. A purity of exactly 99.00% across multiple compounds, or heavy metal results of exactly 0.00 ppm across all analytes, can indicate fabrication.
  • ⚠ PDF metadata shows post-issue editing. PDF metadata (accessible via file properties or free tools like ExifTool) includes creation and modification timestamps. A modification date significantly after the document's stated issue date warrants scrutiny.
  • ⚠ No signature or unverifiable signer. The document lacks an authorized signature, or lists a name that cannot be confirmed as a scientist affiliated with the named laboratory.
  • ⚠ Brand refuses to name the laboratory. A legitimate brand should be able to name the third-party laboratory that tested each lot. Unwillingness to name the lab, or providing only initials, is a material omission.

Why Lot Number Matching Is the Most Critical Step

A COA is a statement about a specific batch of material, not a compound category. Manufacturers produce multiple lots of a given compound over time. Raw material quality, synthesis conditions, potential contaminants, and degradation pathways can all vary between production runs. A passing COA from lot A provides no analytical basis for conclusions about lot B, even if both are the same compound from the same manufacturer.

The lot number is the chain-of-custody link that connects analytical data to specific physical material. Without exact lot number matching, a COA is nothing more than a document that demonstrates a manufacturer can produce a batch that passes testing — not that the batch you actually have passed testing.

The Problem With PDF COAs and Emailed Documents

The standard practice of distributing COAs as PDF files sent by email or shared via Google Drive or Dropbox has a structural security problem: PDF files can be edited, re-saved, and redistributed without any visible indication of tampering. A standard PDF viewer shows no modification history and no chain of custody. The burden of verification falls entirely on the recipient.

This creates a "trust me, here's a PDF" dynamic that benefits bad actors disproportionately. A buyer who cannot verify a document independently is forced to rely on the seller's assurance that the document is authentic — which is precisely the assurance that fraud is designed to provide.

Hosted verification addresses this at the infrastructure level. When a brand publishes its COA data to a permanent, publicly accessible URL — for example, on their own domain at yourbrand.com/verify — a buyer can navigate directly to that URL, type in a lot number, and see the data as published by the brand. No file passes through email. No PDF can be silently swapped out. The URL either returns data for that lot or it does not, with no intermediate layer where tampering can occur undetected.

Summary: COA Verification Checklist

  1. Confirm the lot number on the COA exactly matches the lot number on your physical product label.
  2. Identify the issuing laboratory — full name, address, and accreditation number.
  3. Verify the laboratory's accreditation status through the relevant accrediting body's public registry (A2LA, PJLA, or equivalent), not through the COA document itself.
  4. Confirm an analytical method is named for each key analyte (HPLC, LC-MS/MS, ICP-MS, etc.).
  5. Check that the test date is consistent with the production timeline and does not precede sample receipt.
  6. Inspect PDF metadata for post-issue modification if the document is provided as a file.
  7. Contact the laboratory directly (using independently sourced contact information) to confirm the report number is on file.
  8. Prefer brands that publish COAs to a permanent, publicly accessible hosted URL rather than distributing only PDF files.
